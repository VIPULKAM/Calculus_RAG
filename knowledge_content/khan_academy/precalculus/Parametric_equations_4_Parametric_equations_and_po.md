# Parametric equations 4 | Parametric equations and polar coordinates | Precalculus | Khan Academy

*Source: [Khan Academy Video](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=IReD6c_njOY)*

## Key Concepts
- Parametric equations can represent the same path (like an ellipse) in multiple ways
- Removing the parameter converts parametric equations into a standard Cartesian equation
- Different parametric equations can produce the same shape but with different motion (direction and speed)
- The conversion from parametric to Cartesian form is not reversible (information about motion is lost)

## Definitions
- **Parametric Equations**: Equations where $x$ and $y$ are defined in terms of a third variable (parameter $t$)
- **Ellipse Equation**: $\frac{x^2}{a^2} + \frac{y^2}{b^2} = 1$ where $a$ and $b$ are constants
- **Trigonometric Identity**: $\sin^2(\theta) + \cos^2(\theta) = 1$ for any angle $\theta$

## Examples & Steps

### Example 1: Original Parametric Equations
Given: $x = 3\cos(t)$, $y = 2\sin(t)$

**Step 1 – Solve for trigonometric functions**
- $\frac{x}{3} = \cos(t)$
- $\frac{y}{2} = \sin(t)$

**Step 2 – Apply trigonometric identity**
- $\sin^2(t) + \cos^2(t) = 1$
- $\left(\frac{y}{2}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{x}{3}\right)^2 = 1$

**Step 3 – Convert to standard form**
- $\frac{x^2}{9} + \frac{y^2}{4} = 1$ (ellipse equation)

### Example 2: Different Parametric Equations, Same Path
Given: $x = 3\cos(2t)$, $y = 2\sin(2t)$

**Step 1 – Solve for trigonometric functions**
- $\frac{x}{3} = \cos(2t)$
- $\frac{y}{2} = \sin(2t)$

**Step 2 – Apply trigonometric identity**
- $\sin^2(2t) + \cos^2(2t) = 1$
- $\left(\frac{y}{2}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{x}{3}\right)^2 = 1$

**Step 3 – Convert to standard form**
- $\frac{x^2}{9} + \frac{y^2}{4} = 1$ (same ellipse equation)

**Comparison of Motion**:
- First set ($\cos(t)$, $\sin(t)$): Moves counterclockwise at normal speed
- Second set ($\cos(2t)$, $\sin(2t)$): Moves counterclockwise at twice the speed
- Both trace the same ellipse but with different timing

## Summary
The video showed how different parametric equations can produce the same elliptical path when converted to Cartesian form. However, parametric equations preserve important information about motion (direction and speed) that is lost when converting to standard $x$-$y$ equations. This demonstrates why parametric equations are useful for describing paths where motion characteristics matter.
