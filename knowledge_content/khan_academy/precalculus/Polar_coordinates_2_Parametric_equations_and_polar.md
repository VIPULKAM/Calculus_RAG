# Polar coordinates 2 | Parametric equations and polar coordinates | Precalculus | Khan Academy

*Source: [Khan Academy Video](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zGpbSGj_vfE)*

## Key Concepts
- Converting between polar and rectangular coordinates
- Graphing points and equations in polar coordinates
- Understanding the geometric meaning of polar equations
- How negative radius values work in polar coordinates

## Definitions
- **Polar coordinates**: $(r, θ)$ where:
  - $r$ = distance from origin (radius)
  - $θ$ = angle from positive x-axis (in degrees or radians)

- **Conversion formulas**:
  - $x = r\cosθ$
  - $y = r\sinθ$
  - $r^2 = x^2 + y^2$
  - $\tanθ = \frac{y}{x}$

## Examples & Steps

### Example 1: Convert polar to rectangular coordinates
Convert $(4, 150°)$ to Cartesian coordinates $(x, y)$

**Step 1:** Use conversion formulas
- $x = r\cosθ = 4\cos150°$
- $y = r\sinθ = 4\sin150°$

**Step 2:** Calculate values
- $\cos150° = -\cos30° ≈ -0.866$
- $\sin150° = \sin30° = 0.5$
- $x = 4 × (-0.866) ≈ -3.46$
- $y = 4 × 0.5 = 2$

**Solution:** $(-3.46, 2)$

### Example 2: Convert rectangular equation to polar form
Convert $x^2 + y^2 = 4$ to polar coordinates

**Step 1:** Substitute using $x^2 + y^2 = r^2$
- $r^2 = 4$

**Step 2:** Solve for r
- $r = ±2$

**Interpretation:** This represents a circle with radius 2 centered at the origin. The ± sign doesn't matter because a negative radius means going in the opposite direction of the angle.

### Example 3: Convert another rectangular equation
Convert $x^2 + y^2 = 9\left(\frac{y}{x}\right)^2$ to polar form

**Step 1:** Substitute polar equivalents
- $x^2 + y^2 = r^2$
- $\frac{y}{x} = \tanθ$
- $r^2 = 9(\tanθ)^2$

**Step 2:** Take square root of both sides
- $r = ±3\tanθ$

## Summary
This lesson covered converting between polar and rectangular coordinate systems using fundamental trigonometric relationships. The key insight is that polar coordinates describe points using distance and angle, while Cartesian coordinates use horizontal and vertical positions. Some equations, like circles centered at the origin, become much simpler when expressed in polar form.
